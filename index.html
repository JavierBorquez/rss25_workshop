Towards Reliable and Safe Autonomy

June 25, 2025

University of Southern California, Los Angeles

Robotics: Science and Systems

As autonomous systems are designed to perform increasingly sophisticated tasks in complex environments, ensuring safe and reliable operations of the systems is paramount to their successful deployments. However, this is challenging due to the fact that these systems rely on learning-based methods, including foundation models, for decision-making and control. Ensuring true reliability extends far beyond achieving good performance; it requires rigorous verification and a holistic, multidisciplinary approach encompassing robustness, interpretability, and, importantly, certifiability.

This workshop fosters essential cross-disciplinary collaboration, uniting leading researchers and practitioners in machine learning, planning, and decision-making for the domain of safety-critical applications such as space robotics and autonomous vehicles. We will delve into the requirements and methodologies for constructing safe and reliable autonomous systems, striving to keep pace with the rise of black-box intelligence and the real-world impact of such systems. We plan on discussing the following topics: Industry Insights and Challenges in Developing Safe Autonomous Systems, Safety in Data-Driven Algorithms for Robotics, and Control-Theoretic Approaches for Safety Controller Synthesis.

The core goal of this workshop is to highlight insights emerging from both academic research and industry-driven safety engineering practices through keynote lectures, poster presentations, and round table discussions with active participation by attendees from diverse backgrounds. We aim to bridge the gap between theoretical foundations and practical applications, and as a result, facilitate and contribute to the development of safe, trustworthy, and certifiable autonomous systems.

Call for Papers

We invite researchers and practitioners to submit their papers to the workshop. Submissions should address topics related to safety in autonomous systems, including but not limited to:

  • Robustness against adversarial perturbations
  • Verifiability of decision-making models
  • Interpretability in high-stakes environments
  • Formal methods in certifying autonomous systems
